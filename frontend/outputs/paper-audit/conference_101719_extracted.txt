Response-Level Pedagogical Strategy Selection Using
Contextual Bandits in LLM-Based Tutoring Systems
Amulya S, Jeevan Raj S B, R A Nithin Nandana, Siddesh K R
Department of Information Science and Engineering
RV College of Engineering, Bengaluru
1RV23IS400, 1RV22IS026, 1RV22IS047, 1RV22IS064
Abstract—Large language model (LLM) tutors can generate
explanations, examples, quizzes, and visual learning aids, but
generation quality alone does not determine which pedagogical
form is appropriate for a learner at a given turn. This paper
presents a systems architecture for response-level pedagog-
ical strategy selection in LLM-based tutoring. The system
uses a LinUCB contextual bandit to select among six core
response strategies: visual widget generation, guided step-by-
step explanation, quiz-based checking, concise text explanation,
Socratic questioning, and remediation. The context vector en-
codes eight measurable pre-generation features: cognitive state,
engagement level, topic status, performance trend, confidence,
topic diﬀiculty, previous failures, and response time. Delayed
interaction telemetry is mapped to correctness, engagement,
completion, and retention reward components. The paper
formalizes a closed-loop design that connects context extrac-
tion, bandit selection, prompt enforcement, delayed reward
attribution, persistence, and baseline evaluation. It also reports
a preliminary empirical evaluation with 25 matched participant
pairs, where mean post-test score increased from 3.13/4 to
3.73/4. These results are interpreted cautiously because of
the limited sample size and heuristic scoring of open-ended
responses.

I. Introduction
LLM-based tutors can produce fluent educational re-
sponses, but fluency does not ensure pedagogical appro-
priateness. A policy mechanism is required to determine
whether the next response should be visual, stepwise, quiz-
oriented, concise, Socratic, or remedial. This paper studies
the following question:
Can
contextual
bandits
support
practical
response-level pedagogical strategy selection in
LLM-based tutoring systems under sparse online
feedback?
The work is scoped as a systems contribution with
preliminary empirical evidence. It does not claim definitive
educational superiority. Its central claim is that a contex-
tual bandit can serve as an auditable decision layer that
selects the response form, records the decision context,
checks whether the generated response follows the selected
strategy, and attributes delayed learner feedback to the
original action.
The paper makes three contributions. First, it frames
response-level pedagogical strategy selection as distinct
from curriculum sequencing, exercise recommendation,
and feedback targeting. Prior educational bandit systems
primarily choose what content, exercise, or feedback target
should appear next; this work chooses how the next
LLM response should be structured. Second, it presents a
closed-loop architecture for integrating LinUCB selection
with prompt enforcement and delayed reward attribution.
Third, it specifies an evaluation protocol with operational
metrics, learning metrics, baselines, ablations, and threats
to validity.
Unlike prior educational bandit approaches that adapt
curriculum sequencing or feedback targeting, this work
performs response-level pedagogical adaptation where the
action determines the structure of the generated response
itself.
The following hypotheses define the intended empirical
tests. H1: LinUCB-based strategy selection improves av-
erage operational reward compared with a fixed-strategy
baseline. H2: context-aware selection improves average re-
ward compared with non-contextual or uniformly random
strategy selection. H3: context-aware selection reduces
reward volatility and dropout variance across learner-state
groups. Engagement consistency is defined as reduced vari-
ance in dropout, interaction depth, and reward volatility
across learner states.
II. Related Work
Intelligent tutoring systems use learner models to adapt
instruction. Bloom’s tutoring result motivates person-
alization as an educational goal [1], and meta-analytic
evidence has found positive learning effects for intelligent
tutoring systems across controlled studies [2]. Bayesian
Knowledge Tracing models mastery as a latent state [3],
Deep Knowledge Tracing learns sequential interaction rep-
resentations [4], and the Knowledge-Learning-Instruction
framework links instructional decisions to learning pro-
cesses [5]. These approaches estimate what a learner
knows; the present work focuses on how an LLM tutor
should structure its next response.
Adaptive educational systems have used learner models
for content, navigation, and curriculum adaptation [6].
Contextual bandits provide an online-learning formulation
for repeated decisions under uncertainty [7], and LinUCB
is a standard approach for linear contextual reward models

with exploration bonuses [8]. Existing educational bandit
work has addressed adaptive curriculum assignment and
natural-language feedback targeting [9], [10]. The gap
addressed here is response-level adaptation: the action is
the instructional form of an LLM response, not the next
curriculum item, exercise, or student feedback target.
Dialogue-based tutoring systems such as AutoTutor
demonstrate
that
natural-language
tutoring
can
use
mixed-initiative dialogue and adaptive moves rather than
static answer delivery [12]. Recent LLM education research
investigates tutoring dialogues, feedback generation, per-
sonalized support, and evaluation of tutor behavior [13]–
[16]. MathDial shows that models may solve math prob-
lems while still failing at tutoring moves such as guided
feedback and avoiding premature solution disclosure [17].
Socratic-style LLM tutoring further highlights the need
to choose pedagogical response forms rather than only
produce correct answers [18].
More recent work reinforces this shift toward adap-
tive LLM tutoring and agentic learner modeling. Sur-
veys of LLM agents in education describe pedagogical
agents for feedback, curriculum support, assessment, and
domain-specific tutoring [19]. Large-scale bandit-based
tutoring work has examined how assistance actions can
be optimized over many learners and questions [20].
Reinforcement-learning and learner-state approaches, in-
cluding RL-DKT and multi-agent tutoring systems with
centralized learner models, further motivate auditable
adaptation over persistent learner state [21], [22]. These
studies strengthen the case for separating learner-state
estimation, pedagogical action selection, and response
generation.
III. Methodology and System Design
The method treats each tutoring turn as a strategy-
selection problem. Before generation, the backend derives
a learner context and selects one of six core pedagogical
actions. The selected action is injected into the prompt as
a response-format requirement and checked after genera-
tion. Fig. 1 summarizes the closed-loop pipeline.
The action space is intentionally limited to six core
strategies to reduce sparse-reward fragmentation. Expand-
ing to eight or more actions would increase exploration
cost before suﬀicient reward evidence exists. Table I lists
the implemented actions. Analogy explanation and chal-
lenge mode are reserved as future action-space extensions
after pilot data can support finer distinctions.
The context features are observable before response
generation, lightweight, interpretable, and available be-
fore delayed learning outcomes. Cognitive state is linked
to cognitive load [11]; engagement level approximates
interaction quality; topic status approximates mastery;
performance trend captures recent progress; confidence
combines quiz accuracy or mastery and success rate;
topic diﬀiculty uses a predefined diﬀiculty scale; previous
failures capture repeated incorrect attempts; and response

Contextual bandits are used instead of full reinforce-
ment learning because the decision is short-horizon, re-
wards are sparse and delayed, state transitions are un-
certain, and early deployments provide limited labelled
trajectories. Thompson sampling is a reasonable alterna-
tive but introduces posterior-sampling design choices that
complicate auditability. Neural bandits can model non-
linear reward structure but require more data and higher
serving complexity. LinUCB provides online updates,
interpretable scores, and low computational cost under
cold-start constraints.
V. Architecture
The architecture consists of context extraction, LinUCB
selection, prompt instruction injection, response enforce-
ment, persistence, reward computation, and evaluation
logging. Its primary systems property is traceability: each
generated response can be linked to a context vector,
selected action, action score, enforcement result, and
reward.
Prompt enforcement is required because LLM out-
put can violate policy intent. A quiz_check decision
may generate no question, and a visual_widget deci-
sion may produce only prose. Enforcement checks min-
imal structural compliance rather than pedagogical cor-
rectness. The implemented checks require widget calls
for visual_widget, numbered or bulleted structure for
guided_steps, questions for quiz_check, no forced wid-
get for text_explanation, at least two questions and
no direct-answer marker before the first question for
socratic_questioning, and misconception language plus
corrected simpler restatement for remediation. Fallback
and enforcement-failure rates are logged as reliability
signals.
The reward model is
R = 0.4C + 0.3E + 0.2M + 0.1T,
(9)
where C, E, M, and T denote correctness, engagement,
completion, and retention. The weights were initialized
based on educational priorities: correctness receives the
largest weight because it is closest to demonstrated
learning, engagement and completion are treated as sup-
porting behavioral signals, and retention is included as
a delayed outcome proxy. These values are not claimed
to be optimal; they should be optimized through future
empirical calibration against externally graded learning
outcomes. This design can over-optimize engagement with-
out learning, so future calibration should regress reward
components against external learning outcomes after pilot
data are collected.
Reward telemetry may arrive after widget use, quiz
response, follow-up behavior, or completion. Decision
identifiers link delayed telemetry to the original action
before policy update. Cold-start handling initializes all six
actions with equal priors and increases exploration until
suﬀicient action samples are observed. Stored context vec-
tors include a context-version field; old four-dimensional
pending decisions are skipped during update rather than
applied to the eight-dimensional policy.
For |A| actions and context dimension d, action scoring
requires O(|A|d2) operations when inverse matrices are
cached. The implemented policy uses |A| = 6 and d = 8.
The decision path logs bandit_decision_latency_ms with
selected action, context version, action count, and context
dimension for empirical latency reporting. No average
latency is reported because no deployment log analysis
is included.
VI. Testing and Results
A. Testing Strategy
The proposed evaluation protocol includes user-level as-
signment, baselines, operational metrics, learning metrics,
ablations, and statistical reporting. Users are assigned by
stable hashing. The treatment condition uses LinUCB.
Baselines include fixed guided_steps, uniform random
strategy selection, and a non-contextual bandit.
Operational metrics assess whether the infrastructure
functions as intended: average reward, reward by compo-
nent, action distribution, reward volatility by learner state,
dropout rate, interaction depth, enforcement failure rate,
fallback rate, reward resolution rate, and decision latency.
Learning metrics are required before making educational
claims: pre/post learning gain, delayed retention, time-to-
correct-answer, attempts to correctness, and learner satis-
faction. Statistical reporting should include confidence in-
tervals and effect sizes; skewed reward distributions should
be analyzed with robust regression or non-parametric
tests.
Ablations should test removal of one context feature at
a time, removal of prompt enforcement, random selection
versus LinUCB, contextual versus non-contextual bandits,
and six-action policy performance against a reduced four-
action policy. Fig. 2 summarizes the evaluation design,
and Table II summarizes the evaluation targets.
Table III reports preliminary offline-replay baseline
results from the pilot logs. These values are used as
sanity-check comparisons rather than definitive policy-
performance claims because the pilot was not a fully
randomized controlled deployment.

5) Discussion:
The empirical results indicate three
strengths: adaptive learning support, visualization-based
explanation support, and positive learner engagement.
However, the study has important limitations. The partic-
ipant count remains modest, open-ended responses were
scored using a reusable completeness heuristic rather than
a manually validated grading rubric, and the evaluation
duration was limited. Future work should conduct a larger
study, introduce an automatic grading rubric validated
against human scoring, and evaluate longitudinal retention
over multiple learning sessions.
VII. Threats to Validity and Limitations
Reward bias is the primary validity threat. Engagement
can increase without comprehension, and heuristic weights
may overvalue interaction volume. Telemetry sparsity
can produce unstable reward estimates, especially when
learners abandon responses before feedback is recorded.
Policy collapse may occur if early rewards favor one action.
Exploration can expose struggling learners to unsuitable
strategies. Context-feature limitations remain significant
because cognitive state, confidence, and topic diﬀiculty
are inferred or configured rather than directly observed.
Incorrect cognitive-state inference can route learners to
unsuitable responses.
Privacy and ethics risks arise from learner-state logging.
All participant data were anonymized and collected with
informed consent. The system should collect minimum
necessary telemetry, anonymize exports, define retention
limits, and restrict access to learner traces. Additional
harms include incorrect tutoring advice, over-reliance
on automated support, adaptation bias, and unequal
treatment across learner groups. Conservative defaults
should be used for vulnerable learner states, and educator
review is required before high-stakes deployment. These
limits reinforce the paper’s central boundary: the reported
results are preliminary and should not be interpreted as
definitive evidence of educational superiority.
VIII. Discussion
The main contribution is a deployable structure for
response-level adaptation. The system separates strategy
selection from content generation, makes policy decisions
inspectable, records delayed reward signals, and measures
when LLM output fails to follow policy intent. This
bridge between adaptive policy learning and prompt-
level response control is the paper’s strongest systems
contribution.
The principal weakness is the evidence bottleneck. The
preliminary study suggests promising improvement and
positive learner perception, but the sample is not large
enough to establish generalizable educational effective-
ness. The reward function is heuristic, and the context
vector remains limited to features that can be measured
before generation. The six-action policy is intentionally
conservative under sparse feedback; analogy explanation
and challenge mode are deferred until pilot data justify a
larger action space.
IX. Conclusion
This paper addressed the problem of selecting the
pedagogical form of an LLM tutor response under sparse
online feedback. It presented a LinUCB-based architec-
ture for six response-level strategies, eight-feature context
extraction, prompt enforcement, delayed reward attribu-
tion, cold-start handling, latency logging, and baseline
evaluation. The preliminary empirical findings indicate
promising learning improvement and user satisfaction,
with mean score increasing from 3.13/4 to 3.73/4 among
25 matched participant pairs. These findings should be
interpreted cautiously because of the modest participant
count, heuristic scoring, and limited evaluation duration.

Future work should run larger controlled studies, calibrate
reward weights against learning outcomes, and evaluate
context, enforcement, and action-space ablations.
